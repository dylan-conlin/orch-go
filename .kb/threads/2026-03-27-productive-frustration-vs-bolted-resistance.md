# Productive frustration vs bolted-on resistance — when is frustration a comprehension gap vs a system failure?

## 2026-03-27

Empirical finding from 18 session debriefs: high-friction sessions produce 2.4x more learnings than zero-friction sessions (16.6 vs 6.8 average). The frustration boundary design assumed all frustration is bad — trigger a restart. But the data says the opposite: the most productive sessions in the system's history (Mar 10, 11, 12, 19, 20) were ALL high-friction sessions.

Two types emerge from the data. Bolted-on frustration: governance hooks blocking valid work, tool quirks, process ceremony exceeding the fix — system fighting you, no new understanding forming, fix count rises. Just-hard frustration: formalization struggle, publication direction abandonment, measurement conceptualization — productive struggle, understanding forming even if it hurts, fix count falls.

The discriminating signal isn't the frustration itself but what's happening alongside it: are artifacts forming? Are phases advancing? Is the fix count going up (bolted-on) or down (just-hard)? The frustration boundary detector needs a "productivity qualifier" — check artifact creation, phase transitions, and fix-count direction before triggering. This is required for interactive sessions (interrupting a breakthrough is costly) and optional for headless workers (respawn is cheap).

Open question: can the system surface the TYPE of resistance (bolted-on vs just-hard) as an ambient signal in the product surface? The data exists in beads issue chains and area label concentration. Dylan described wanting this: "the system should distinguish 'bolted-on pattern' (4 fixes in 3 weeks, each spawning follow-ups) from 'just-hard pattern' (1 hard fix that stayed solved)."
