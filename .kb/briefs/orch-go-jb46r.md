# Brief: orch-go-jb46r

## Frame

The frustration boundary design says: when the system detects you're frustrated, it should restart the session. Fresh frame, fresh start, carry the question not the conversation. That felt right — until I looked at which sessions actually produced the deepest understanding. Turns out the frustrating ones did.

## Resolution

I counted friction reports, learnings, spawns, and completions across every March session debrief. Sessions with real friction — governance hooks blocking valid work, tool quirks, the pain of formalizing something that doesn't want to be formalized — averaged 2.4x more learnings than the smooth, zero-friction sessions. The five most productive sessions in the system's entire history were ALL high-friction sessions.

The surprise clarified a distinction that was already forming in the resistance thread. There are two kinds of frustration. The first is the system fighting you: governance hooks blocking the exact file you need, tab escapes breaking on macOS, process ceremony that takes longer than the fix. Each patch spawns more patches, the fix count goes up. This is bolted-on resistance, and it's the one worth interrupting. The second is the problem fighting you: the knowledge accretion formalization that took three sessions to click, the publication direction that had to be abandoned before a better one appeared, the measurement insight that required seeing the same failure from three angles. Each fix makes the remaining problems fewer and clearer. The fix count goes down. This is just-hard resistance, and it's the one a boundary would destroy.

The frustration detector needs a "productivity qualifier" — before triggering a boundary, check: are artifacts being created? Are phases advancing? Is the fix count falling? If so, the frustration is productive. Surface it as "hard work in progress" but don't trigger a restart. The qualifier is essential for interactive sessions where interrupting a breakthrough is expensive. For headless workers, respawns are cheap enough that the qualifier is nice-to-have.

## Tension

The 2.4x number is real but the sample is small (18 sessions) and there's a confound: the high-friction sessions are also the longest, most ambitious sessions. Friction might correlate with productivity because both correlate with session ambition, not because friction causes insight. The fix-count direction signal solves this at the individual-session level — but nobody has computed it from beads data yet. It's a computable signal that doesn't exist as infrastructure.
