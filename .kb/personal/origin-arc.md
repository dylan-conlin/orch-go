## The Tool Progression (Understanding Inversely Correlated with Capability)

- **Pre-LLM:** Web dev, copying from StackOverflow, keeping solution patterns in org-mode/Roam. Already had the instinct to externalize knowledge — org-mode was a manual KB.
- **ChatGPT 3.0/3.5:** Could sense the power early. Conversational style (gptel in Emacs) was slow but produced understanding — copy/paste forced comprehension.
- **Aider:** Less copy/pasting was faster, but the first feeling of "understanding starting to slip" appeared here. Conversation → delegation removed a comprehension checkpoint.
- **Claude Code:** "The real understanding pains started setting in because it was so good at what it does."

**The turn:** Each tool improvement made Dylan faster and less comprehending. The story everyone tells is "AI made me 10x." The cost nobody talks about: understanding degrades as delegation increases.

## The Black Box Moment (SendCutSend)

New job + new coding tool simultaneously. Working through features faster than ever, but couldn't explain what he'd built. The vocabulary to describe his own work was missing.

**Defining experience:** Built a complex Excel-to-SQL migration that was beyond the engineering team's skillset. Got it working by having AI extract verification targets from the spreadsheet, then used those to validate the giant SQL. It worked. He could prove it worked. He couldn't explain *why* or *how* it worked.

"Living in this reality is something I'm getting better at, but when I don't have verification targets I struggle."

**This is the seed of everything:** Harness engineering, completion verification, measurement honesty — all trace back to "I need something external that tells me whether this is right, because I can't tell by reading it." The agent problem (correct work without coherence verification) started as Dylan's own problem.
