  Cursor's Math Breakthrough

  Cursor's CEO Michael Truell announced that Cursor discovered a novel solution 
  to Problem Six of the First Proof Challenge — a set of research-grade math
  problems designed to approximate the work of Stanford, MIT, and Berkeley
  academics. The problem was in spectral graph theory.

  What happened

  - Cursor ran their general-purpose coding agent harness — the same one they
  used to build a browser from scratch and do massive code migrations — against
  this math research problem
  - It ran autonomously for 4 days with no human guidance, no domain-specific
  modifications, and no mathematical reasoning machinery
  - The result: Cursor's solution yields stronger results than the official, 
  human-written solution

  Why it matters for harness engineering

  The key insight isn't "AI can do math." It's about harnesses:

  - The architecture is hierarchical: planners (explore and create tasks, spawn
  sub-planners), workers (execute independently), and judge agents (evaluate
  correctness)
  - The pattern is: decompose, parallelize, verify, iterate — four independent
  organizations converged on this same architecture
  - Prompt engineering mattered more than architecture: "The harness and models
  matter, but the prompts matter more"
  - The system scaled to hundreds of concurrent agents, trillions of tokens, 1M+
   lines of code across 1,000 files in coding tasks

  The structural claim

  When a coding harness outperforms a domain-specific math agent, the insight is
   about the generality of the harness pattern, not about math capability. The
  "jagged frontier" (where AI is great at some tasks and terrible at adjacent
  ones) was a measurement artifact — it described what happens when you remove
  organizational structure and request single answers. Add structure
  (decompose/parallelize/verify/iterate), and the frontier smooths out.

  This is directly relevant to your harness engineering thread — Cursor's result
   is empirical evidence that enforcement + measurement + iteration structure
  generalizes beyond the domain it was built for.

  Cursor and we are not disagreeing — we're talking about different things at 
  different scales.

  Cursor's claim: "prompts matter more than architecture" comes from their
  context of hundreds of concurrent agents working on a single codebase for 
  weeks. Their "harness" is the planner/worker/judge hierarchy + the prompts
  that drive those roles. When they say "prompts," they mean the instructions
  that shape how agents decompose, parallelize, verify, and iterate. Their
  architecture (the hierarchical multi-agent structure) is relatively fixed —
  the gains come from tuning what those agents are told to do.

  Our model says the opposite: soft harness (instructions, skills, CLAUDE.md) 
  dilutes under pressure, and hard harness (gates, structural tests, pre-commit 
  hooks) is what actually holds. 265 contrastive trials showed behavioral
  constraints become inert at 10+ items. daemon.go grew +892 lines despite
  CLAUDE.md saying the limit was 1,500.

  But here's the tension worth sitting with:

  1. Cursor operates at greenfield scale with disposable output. They built a
  browser from scratch, solved a math problem. If an agent produces garbage,
  another agent verifies and rejects it. The verify/iterate loop IS their
  enforcement — but it's soft (judge agent prompts), not hard (pre-commit
  hooks). They can afford this because they're not maintaining a living
  codebase.
  2. We operate on a living system where mistakes compound. 30 agents over 60
  days, each locally correct, produced +2,100 lines of duplicated
  infrastructure. Our problem isn't "did the agent produce a correct answer" —
  it's "did 30 agents preserve structural coherence across 6,000 commits."
  Cursor doesn't face this problem because their agents build fresh and throw
  away.
  3. The real claim difference: Cursor says prompts > architecture for task 
  completion quality. We say architecture > prompts for multi-agent structural 
  coherence over time. These aren't contradictory — they're about different
  failure modes. Cursor optimizes for compliance (did the agent do the right
  thing). We optimize for coordination (did N agents compose well together).

  The thread from your harness model nails this: compliance failure vs
  coordination failure. Cursor's math result is a compliance triumph — one agent
   (or agent swarm) solving one bounded problem correctly. Our pain is
  coordination failure — individually correct agents composing into structural
  degradation.

  The question this raises: Is Cursor's verify/iterate loop actually a soft
  harness that works at their scale because the output is disposable? And does
  it break down exactly where we'd predict — on long-lived codebases where

❯ do you think our harness would be able to solve one of these math problems?   

⏺ No. And the reasons why are interesting.

  Our harness is optimized for the wrong thing. We built enforcement
  infrastructure — gates that prevent bad outcomes (accretion, duplication,
  structural degradation). A math problem doesn't need prevention. It needs
  exploration, verification, and iteration. Our 15-gate completion pipeline,
  spawn hotspot gates, accretion limits — none of that helps you explore a
  spectral graph theory problem space.

  What Cursor's harness has that ours doesn't:

  - Judge agents — agents whose sole job is evaluating whether other agents'
  output is correct. This is the verify/iterate loop. We have completion
  verification (did the agent follow process), not output verification (is the
  answer right).
  - Decomposition as a first-class operation — planners that break a problem
  into independently-attackable subproblems. Our orchestrator triages and
  routes, but doesn't decompose problems. We assign whole problems to whole
  agents.
  - Iteration without completion — their agents can try, fail, try again for 4
  days. Our agents complete or get abandoned. There's no "keep exploring" mode.

  What we have that's irrelevant to math:

  - Accretion gates, duplication detection, hotspot enforcement — all about
  codebase health over time
  - Beads tracking, workspace isolation — coordination infrastructure for a
  living system
  - Skill content transfer, SPAWN_CONTEXT — domain routing, not domain
  exploration

  What would need to change to solve math problems:

  The core pattern Cursor uses — decompose, parallelize, verify, iterate — is
  actually simple. The hard part is the verifier. For code, you can run tests.
  For math, you need an agent that can evaluate proof correctness. That's where
  "prompts matter more" makes sense — the judge agent's prompt is what
  determines whether garbage gets rejected or accepted.

  Our system could probably be extended to do it — orch spawn already handles
  parallel agents, and you could build a judge/verifier pattern on top. But it
  would use almost none of our existing enforcement infrastructure. You'd
  basically be building Cursor's harness from scratch inside our coordination
  layer.

  The honest version: our harness is a maintenance harness (keep a living system
   healthy under continuous agent pressure). Cursor built an exploration harness
   (throw compute at a bounded problem until you find the answer). Different
  tools for different failure modes. Ours prevents coordination failure. Theirs
  maximizes compliance on bounded tasks.

  The deeper question this surfaces: should orch support both modes? An
  "exploration mode" where the gates and enforcement step aside, and the system
  becomes decompose/parallelize/verify/iterate on a bounded problem?

❯ shouldn't it support both modes? how could an exploration mode help us? how   
could it hurt? what are the implications of an exploration mode?                

⏺ Good question. Let me think through this properly.

  How exploration mode helps:

  - Investigation quality. Right now investigations are single-agent,
  single-pass. An investigation that could decompose "why does daemon.go keep
  regrowing" into 5 parallel probes with a judge synthesizing findings would
  produce better models than one agent reading code for 30 minutes.
  - Architect quality. Design decisions are currently one agent's opinion.
  Decompose into competing approaches, let each be explored in parallel, judge
  evaluates tradeoffs. That's closer to how good architecture actually works —
  adversarial exploration of the design space.
  - Unblocks research skill. We have a research skill that's basically
  "investigation but for external topics." It's weak because it's single-pass.
  Exploration mode would make it actually useful — multiple agents exploring
  different angles, synthesizing.
  - Math/reasoning tasks become possible. Not that we need to solve spectral
  graph theory, but "explore whether this abstraction is correct" or "find
  counterexamples to this claim" are things we already want to do (falsification
   probes). Right now probes are single-shot. An exploration probe that iterates
   until it finds a counterexample or exhausts the search space is strictly
  better.

  How it hurts:

  - Cost. Cursor ran hundreds of agents for 4 days on one problem. Trillions of
  tokens. Our system runs on Claude Max subscriptions with rate limits.
  Exploration mode at Cursor's scale would burn through accounts in hours. The
  economics are fundamentally different — they likely have API access at scale
  pricing.
  - Accretion risk. If exploration agents produce code artifacts (not just
  analysis), those artifacts bypass our maintenance harness. An exploration
  agent that "tries 5 approaches to refactor daemon.go" could produce exactly
  the kind of uncoordinated mess our gates exist to prevent. The boundary
  between "exploring a solution" and "implementing a solution" is blurry.
  - Verification is the hard part we don't have. Cursor's judge agents work
  because code can be tested and math can be checked. For our domain — "is this
  architecture decision good?" — verification is judgment, not execution. A
  judge agent evaluating whether an architect's recommendation is sound is
  just... another opinion. We'd need to figure out what "verification" means for
   design exploration, and it's not obvious.
  - Complexity. Two modes means two sets of assumptions about what agents are
  doing. Our completion pipeline, workspace management, beads tracking — all
  assume the maintenance model. Supporting exploration means either a parallel
  infrastructure or making the existing one modal. Both are expensive.
  - The 5-minute rule breaks. Our orchestrator has a hard norm: >5 minutes of
  context gathering = spawn a worker. Exploration mode blurs this. Is the
  orchestrator exploring or investigating? The role boundary between
  orchestrator and worker gets fuzzy.

  The implications I'd actually worry about:

  The deepest risk is mode confusion. An agent that thinks it's exploring (try
  things, iterate, no penalty for failure) but is operating on the living
  codebase (where every commit compounds) would be the worst of both worlds.
  Cursor's exploration works because the output is disposable until it's
  verified. Our codebase is not disposable.

  So exploration mode would need hard isolation — worktrees or scratch
  directories where exploration agents can try anything without touching the
  main codebase. Output only gets promoted to the real codebase after passing
  through the maintenance harness. Exploration produces candidates; maintenance
  gates filter them.

  That's actually a clean architecture: exploration is upstream of enforcement.
  Explore freely in isolation, then the existing gate pipeline decides what gets
   in. The two modes aren't alternatives — they're sequential stages.

  The practical starting point would be narrow: exploration mode for
  investigations and architect work only (analysis, not code production). No
  codebase writes. Just parallel decomposition with synthesis. That gets us
  better thinking without the accretion risk. Code-producing exploration (like
  Cursor's browser build) would be a later, harder problem.

  Want to go deeper on the isolation architecture, or does this change how
  you're thinking about the investigation/architect skills?
